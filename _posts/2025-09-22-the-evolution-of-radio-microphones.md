## The Legends That Built Broadcasting

Some microphones transcend their technical specifications to become true industry legends. The **Electro-Voice RE20** stands as the undisputed champion of professional radio broadcasting, maintaining its dominance for over 50 years since its 1968 debut. This isn't just marketing hype. Walk into virtually any major radio station today, and you'll find RE20s capturing the voices that define our daily commutes.

What makes this microphone so special? The [complete story on Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electro-Voice_RE20) reveals how its revolutionary Variable-D technology eliminated proximity effect, ensuring consistent sound whether hosts lean in for intimate moments or back away for authoritative delivery. It's the kind of technical innovation that seems simple in retrospect but required genuine engineering brilliance to achieve.

Equally fascinating is the **Shure SM7**, documented in detail on [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shure_SM7). This microphone boasts an incredible dual legacy. It captured Michael Jackson's vocals on "Thriller" in 1982 and now dominates the modern podcasting landscape as the SM7B. From the King of Pop to Joe Rogan, this microphone has quite literally shaped the sound of popular culture.

## Inside the Industry: How the Pros Really Work

Ever wondered what microphones your favorite NPR shows actually use? The [NPR Training article "Which mic do they use?"](https://training.npr.org/2016/07/25/which-mic-do-they-use/) pulls back the curtain on professional public radio operations. You might be surprised to learn that even at the highest levels of broadcasting, the choice often comes down to the same reliable workhorses we've been discussing.

NPR's approach illustrates something crucial about professional audio: it's not always about having the most expensive equipment, but rather choosing the right tool for each specific job. Their flagship productions may use $3,400 Neumann U87 microphones, but much of their daily programming relies on the trusty Shure KSM32 at $500. This proves that professional results don't always require premium prices.

## The Bigger Picture: Why This History Matters

This isn't just nostalgia for vintage audio gear. Understanding the evolution of radio microphones reveals how technological constraints shaped the very sound of mass media. Those massive 1930s ribbon microphones didn't just capture sound differently. They literally forced radio hosts to develop the stationary, formal presentation style that defined early broadcasting.

The introduction of more flexible dynamic microphones in the 1950s coincided with rock and roll's emergence, enabling the energetic DJ personalities that helped spread popular music. The intimate sound of 1970s FM radio became possible only after condenser microphones allowed hosts to back away from their mics while maintaining audio quality.

Today's podcast boom builds directly on these technological foundations, with creators accessing professional-quality equipment that would have cost tens of thousands of dollars just decades ago. Every time you hear that warm, authoritative "radio voice," you're experiencing the cumulative effect of nearly a century of acoustic engineering and broadcast innovation.
